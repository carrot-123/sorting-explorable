Sorting with Computers

What is Sorting

If you've ever arranged your books by color, grouped poker cards by suits, or displayed your seashell collection by size, then you have sorted! Sorting simply refers to re-arranging elements in a certain order, and it is something computers have to do very often. Nice job! What are some strategies you used when deciding whether to swap or look at a shell? Chances are, the strategies you used are similar to what a computer would use.

Computers must follow a set of rules that determine a specific order of "looking" and "swapping" (aka an algorithm). There exist many different sorting algorithms, each with their own unique strategy. Let's take a look at three of them, and compare how well you did!

Bubble Sort

First up, it's bubble sort! All shells are visible so that you can see the bigger picture. The shell you are currently looking at is highlighted. Sorting algorithms tend to look at the shells in order, so pressing next will look at the next shell. Additionally, computers, like us, can memorize certain shells it has looked at. In this algorithm, you can look ahead to the next shell, memorize it, and compare it to your current shell. Follow the bubble sort algorithm step-by-step to sort all the seashells.

Algorithm

For as long as the seashells are not sorted, do the following:
  1. If the shell you are looking at is bigger than the next shell, swap them
  2. Look at the next shell
