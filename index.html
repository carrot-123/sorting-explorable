Sorting with Computers

What is Sorting

If you've ever arranged your books by color, grouped poker cards by suits, or displayed your seashell collection by size, then you have sorted! Sorting simply refers to re-arranging elements in a certain order. Here's a quick example of why computers need to sort: Try finding and clicking on "aadvark" for both computers screens below. On which computer was it easier to find?

How we Sort

Sorting *seems* straightforward. Try sorting the seashells below by clicking on two shells to swap their places. Sort from smallest to biggest.
Nice job! For computers, however, sorting is not so trivial. Computers don't have eyes like we do, so they can't see the seashells all at once. Rather, they can only focus on one or two at a time. Let's now try sorting as though we are a computer.

How computers sort

Here, you can only see two seashells at a time: the shell in the spot you are focusing on, and the shell in the spot after it. At any moment, you can swap two shells. To focus on the next shell, press "Next". If you reach the last spot, pressing "Next" will bring you back to first spot. (Hint: You'll most likely have to loop back to the first spot many times.) Try sorting the seashells now!
Nice! As you can see, computers sort differently than humans! Let's explore three different sorting algorithms we can use to arrange our seashells.

Bubble Sort

First up, it's bubble sort! The set-up is exactly the same as the previous sort: you can see the shell in the current spot and the shell in the next spot. At anytime, you can swap shells. Follow the alogirthm (rules) of bubble sort step-by-step to sort all the seashells. (The spot you are focusing on is marked in red. Toggling will mark in green which spot you can swap shells with.)

Algorithm

For as long as the seashells are not sorted, do the following:
  1. If the shell in the current spot is bigger than the shell in the next spot, then swap them
  2. Move on to the next shell
